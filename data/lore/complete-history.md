# Triboro — Complete History

> This is the master canon for Triboro. When anything conflicts, this document
> wins. The long post/voice libraries that used to live here have moved to
> [`sample-posts.md`](sample-posts.md) so the feed generator can draw on them.
> See [`README.md`](README.md) for how the canon is layered.

## Premise

Triboro is a narrative, puzzle-based comedy-adventure game. The player is a
hacker-for-hire surfing the social network of a small, bizarre community in
Western Pennsylvania. Missions involve stealing secrets, files, and information
from targets on the social network through phishing schemes and digital ruses.
As the player clears increasingly complex hacking jobs, they learn more about
the strange world of Triboro, and its true nature is gradually revealed.

## Setting

The game takes place inside the dedicated social network of a large apartment
complex in Western Pennsylvania: **Triboro Apartments**.

Many years ago, a massive industrial/environmental disaster covered the
surrounding area with toxic smog, forcing the residents to shelter inside and
wait for the air to clear. The air never cleared.

Today's residents are generations removed from the disaster. In isolation they
have developed their own religions, rituals, and political culture. They call
the surrounding smog **"The Aurora"** and never discuss leaving the building.

When the game begins, two factions — **The League of Trades** and **Kirwin
Foods** — are locked in a struggle for control of the complex's fractious
government and limited resources. The population has come to accept a
near-constant state of disasters and sectarian conflict. As a hacker-for-hire
scraping out a living, the player picks up work from both factions and from
ordinary citizens in need of their particular skills.

## The Almanapp

The Almanapp is where the player finds targets and reads the world. Following
new developments in the feed and understanding this strange place is the key to
hacking targets and advancing.

It is a bush-league version of Facebook built for a single apartment complex.
Beyond personal profiles, it hosts pages for businesses, community groups,
government announcements, and journalism. It is the only source of digital news
and online commerce in Triboro.

Once the solo project of the building owner's nephew, the Almanapp is now run by
the beleaguered, underfunded IT department of the Triboro government — three
people in their late 60s, because government work is deeply unattractive to the
young. It has community standards forbidding insults and falsehoods; the most
extreme members of both factions, and the general population, violate them
constantly. When the game begins, the IT department has just launched a small,
pathetic public-awareness campaign about the risks of hacking on the platform —
these appear as PSAs that everyone trashes.

The Almanapp is also a main venue for entertainment. The marketplace sells
ebooks. Both the League and Kirwin's run dueling repertory theater companies
that travel the building, and their rivalry becomes a plot point. Other troupes
do freak shows and long-running serials; by their titles, many productions seem
to involve blood sports or burlesque. Ebooks are mostly Tom-Clancy-style
thrillers and steamy romance novels set inside Triboro. Gambling on the outcomes
of plays and famous novels is a proud building-wide pastime.

The other main entertainment is youth sports — lacrosse, track and field, and
ultimate fighting most of all. Youth sports have replaced adult sports in the
hearts of Triboro's adults. Dominant pee-wee athletes are covered like pros, and
gambling on youth sports is a proud national pastime.

## News Media

Three outlets are visible at the start. Over the game, each becomes a worse
version of itself or closes entirely: Babbage moves from hard news to clickbait,
one faction seizes control of one outlet, and the third shuts down completely.

- **The Babbage Family Press** — the legacy outlet, a USA-Today equivalent.
  Every byline is a member of the extended Babbage family. Starts out with a
  straight, "radical moderate" style and constantly reminds readers of its long
  history of service. (Named Babbages: Sarah, senior meteorologist; Ward,
  security correspondent; Emma.)
- **The Community Newsletter** — community and business news, widely seen as a
  government mouthpiece. A small-town Bloomberg that worships money and status
  and always compliments the government and police.
- **TB Alerts** — lifestyle, homecare, and breaking news; the New York Post of
  this world. Heavy on sports, gambling, and salacious rumors about faction and
  government leaders.

## Arts & Culture

Decades of isolation have produced a rich, profitable homegrown culture.

- **Fiction dominates.** Episodic installments of long-running serial novels are
  discussed constantly on the Almanapp — wild fantasies and spy thrillers, all
  set inside Triboro.
- **Shannon Wascombe** is the Tom Clancy of this world: militaristic thrillers
  that glorify weapons and police. Her flagship series, **"Dutch Melton,"**
  follows a wily Security Branch Special Forces detective (no such department
  exists). She's a local celebrity who weighs in on "national security."
- **Romance** — a line of erotically charged novels about the Triboro council
  and news media. Like "The West Wing" set in an apartment complex, with far
  more sex. Both the thrillers and the romances sell a fantasy of youth,
  competence, and optimism that clashes with the building's reality.
- **Historical fiction** is big and hotly contested. Former Chief Executive
  **Rudy Glover** is the subject of many biographies and appreciations.
- **Live performance** is huge. The theater district's acts grow more extreme
  over the game as performers try to shock a jaded public. Rehearsed troupes
  steadily lose market share to people who just scream conspiracy theories.
- **Piracy** plagues the theater district — performers reading from novels and
  newspapers they don't own. Official media companies sign open letters
  demanding a crackdown (Act 1). In Act 2 the Security Branch and private forces
  clear out the bad actors, longtime fans complain the scene's gone tame, and a
  "black market theater district" springs up for stolen and disreputable
  content.
- **Talk radio** — recap shows on shortwave and the building intranet dissect
  the serial novels. Sponsored content from Kirwin's and the League seeps into
  all of it over time.

## The Factions

Every NPC belongs to one of five factions.

### The League of Trades

Headquartered in the basement. Descended from the building's original
maintenance staff, they keep the electricity, plumbing, and general upkeep
running, and think of themselves as a class of samurai-like masters — which
clashes with the building's obvious disrepair. Their culture is deeply rust
belt: a militant Teamsters local crossed with an Elk's Lodge. They are
frequently drunk; their parties and functions (company picnics, board meetings)
cause extensive property damage.

Divided into **Water, Electric, Engineering, and Special Ops** departments.
Strict hierarchy, historically ruled by a single charismatic **League
President**. The current president is **Doris Shemp**, a former plumber. They
post frequent relocation notices, since their work often forces residents to
flee their homes for extended periods.

- Raised with a mix of technical knowledge and spiritual reverence for machinery
  and for their own organization.
- They keep a pantheon of famous electricians / handymen / HVAC specialists.
- Locked in a permanent power struggle with Kirwin Foods.
- Spiritual bent; they love glittering platitudes about the soul of the walls.
- A group of zealots never leaves the basement, and only tends the machines.
- Slogans: *Servants of the Great Machine* · *Through Maintenance, We Aspire* ·
  *We Light the Dark* · *Saboteurs Lurk in Shadows.*

### Kirwin Foods

Began as a community garden and volunteer rationing committee; now one of the
most powerful institutions in Triboro. Governed by a five-member **Harvest
Board** on staggered three-year terms. Divisions: **Cultivation, Distribution,
Research** (plus a sizable security team). They fuse a pagan, earth-worshiping
cult with the structure and culture of a Whole Foods corporation — Amazon meets
The Wicker Man. Proudly bureaucratic and business-minded while worshiping The
Earth, or at least the parts of it they can reach from inside Triboro.

Beyond food they've expanded into consumer goods, home delivery, and banking.
Their delivery corps is a glue-sniffing mess; their banking arm (Kirwin's
Savings & Loan) is a corrupt disaster. They brand their store scrip **"Kredits."**

- Control the food supply: hydroponic facilities, fish aquariums, factory farms
  of pigeons and rabbits.
- Fetishize being on the cutting edge and treat it as a divine right.
- Push highly questionable science about what food is nutritious.
- Militantly against private cultivation: *"Remember, private cultivation is A
  CRIME."*
- Manage food/supply airdrops that land on the roof; the building is incurious
  about where it comes from.
- Constantly try to pay workers in company Kredits and believe company policy is
  above the law.
- **Old Man Kirwin** is a Colonel-Sanders-like figure in their advertising. When
  the player hacks Kirwin's private communications, he turns out to be an
  incredibly old, diminished man still calling the shots.
- Constantly spinning outbreaks of food-borne disease tied to their products.
- Slogans: *Happiness Grows Here* · *Consumption is Virtue* · *Why Make When You
  Can Take?*

### The Security Branch

The widely disliked police force. Grew from the pre-Event private security team
and community watch into a quasi-military, quasi-social-services organization.
Disliked for incompetence, violence, and heavy-handed tactics. Currently running
**"Operation Friend Force,"** an ongoing, repeatedly backfiring image campaign.
Both the League and Kirwin's justify their private militias by pointing at how
hated and useless the SB is.

- Poorly trained but relatively well-equipped; siege mentality; treats all
  residents as potential threats.
- Struggles to recruit — resorts to press-ganging residents or accepting
  clearly unsuitable candidates. Has an officer's union that attempts political
  action.
- Their **Chief** is always trying to sell his handicrafts on the marketplace —
  a hook for a later phishing scam.
- Most operations are about the appearance of control, not solving problems. By
  the end they've largely given up on peacekeeping and just guard the few
  remaining government offices.
- Every SB post gets relentless hate and downvotes regardless of content.
- Slogans: *Respect all, suspect all* · *Emotional Support and Physical
  Restraint* · *Give Security a Second Chance.*

### The Aurora Cult

Began as small, non-denominational community events run by a Seventh-Day
Adventist minister, a Muslim dentist, and a group of older men from the
Unitarian church's house band. Over time the smog replaced their old gods, and
the Cult became the conduit to the new ones. At its height it had followers on
every floor, real influence in both the League and Kirwin's, and ran schools and
charities.

Ostentatious wealth and a series of scandals gutted its standing. At game start
it is a husk: rebranding efforts fall flat, it's selling off old properties, its
youth group just imploded, and the "Aurora Schools" are a running joke. Run by a
pastor and a council of deacons — currently without enough deacons to pass new
bylaws. Clearly broke, and drifting toward a casino/lifestyle brand.

- Worship the toxic fog as a divine entity.
- Their holy text describes their home as a large building surrounded by smog,
  but they read it as metaphor.
- Believe the Aurora protects them from a worse outside evil.

### The Triboro Council

The beleaguered five-person government plus an ever-shrinking staff of civil
servants. After the Event the Council ruled hard, then briefly through
communitarian, distributed decision-making — until the **Great Flood** drowned
several floors in grey water and discredited it. In the power vacuum, Kirwin's
and the League rose as rival civic authorities.

Upcoming **elections** to seat new council members are a main plot line. Both
factions spend vast sums lobbying and threatening the Council, openly bribing
both sides with ridiculous gifts, then escalating to overt pressure. **One
council member mysteriously disappears mid-game, each side blaming the other.**
A recurring gag: the Council's total inability to meet the day's problems, and
the advanced age and muddled decision-making of its civil servants. Both
factions bankroll rival "think tanks" that produce studies backing their
preferred policies.

## Main Story Arc

Every playthrough is different, but always moves through three acts.

**Act 1 — Introductions (first 3–5 missions).** The player meets the League and
Kirwin's through the feed. Both organizations post mostly positive material —
new projects, self-promotion — and their conflict is implied, not stated. The
Aurora is introduced, also positively: daily "Aurora Reports" read "Green," and
the Cult gives thanks. Missions are simple: get a target's name, do cursory
research, win a chat. First mentions of **"The Shadow Boy"** and **"The Face in
the Aurora."** Milestones: first government get-out-the-vote posts; the player
unlocks one new hacking tool; first hints of someone outside the building.

**Act 2 — Rising Action (next 3–5 missions).** Things heat up. Missions come from
"anonymous" clients clearly tied to one faction; the player must build fake
profiles and post in public forums to earn targets' trust. Both organizations
strain to stay positive while fighting negative coverage — Kirwin's suppressing
disease-outbreak and bank-run reports, the League denying the building is falling
apart (eventually suspending maintenance reports and blaming terrorists for
abusing the system). Both actively recruit militias. The Aurora worsens to
"Yellow" and "Orange," and residents trade tips for "heavy Aurora days."
Sightings of **"The Shadow Man" / "The Beast."** The act ends with a public armed
battle between Kirwin's and the League, and the player's first multi-step hack.

**Act 3 — All Hell Breaks Loose.** The player's success has ruinous
consequences. The League and Kirwin's fall into open conflict across multiple
floors. A subplot: deserters from both sides form a breakaway commune, quickly
crushed in a rare show of unity. As the war escalates, the public tunes it out
and sinks deeper into pop culture and gambling. The Aurora becomes a full crisis;
wings of the building are evacuated. The climax: an alert says the player has
finally won the jacket they've coveted all game — they click the link, and
everything goes dark. The public demands violent action against "The Beast,"
vigilantism skyrockets, and both factions compete to be seen handling it.
